# Grammaticality needs more than dependency locality

**Brett Reynolds** (ORCID: 0000-0003-0073-7195)
Humber Polytechnic & University of Toronto
[brett.reynolds@humber.ca](mailto:brett.reynolds@humber.ca)

---

Gibson (2026) argues that dependency grammar is "the simplest theory of grammar." The case makes two claims. The first is representational: a language's syntax consists of "just the set of dependency rules," with "no phrase structure or transformation rules." The second is explanatory: dependency locality serves as the unifying principle behind processing effects and crosslinguistic word-order regularities.

But simplest for what? Dependency grammar is strongest where Gibson is most modest: as a perspicuous formalism for word-to-word linkage. It's weakest where the title is most ambitious: as a theory of grammar.

Much of what Gibson argues is well established. Dependency locality has robust support as an efficiency pressure in usage and typology, with evidence that grammars cluster on short-dependency orders (Futrell, Mahowald, & Gibson, 2015). His LLM-learnability critique has real force against poverty-of-the-stimulus arguments that presuppose transformational derivations.

The evidence shows that locality matters. It doesn't show that locality is all that matters. The broader title claim goes beyond what the locality literature itself supports. Futrell, Levy, and Gibson (2020) decline to claim that "dependency grammar is the only correct description of syntax" (p. 373); Hahn, Jurafsky, and Futrell (2020) suggest that dependency-length minimization is downstream of more general parseability and predictability pressures, "without a need for further constraints" (p. 5).

For present purposes, grammaticality concerns the licensed form–meaning pairings of a speech community. Processing viability contributes to what is licensed, but so does what the community has conventionalized over time, and those two contributions need not coincide (Reynolds & Miller, 2026).

Licensing can turn on form–meaning conventions that tree geometry doesn't by itself encode. Consider the English string *I have 57 years*. It reads naturally as a statement of duration, answering a question like "How long until you get out?", but as an age reading, answering "How old are you?", it is unavailable in contemporary Standard English. The duration construal is licensed under contextual recovery; the age construal is not. The dependency skeleton is the same across the two readings: same string, same word-to-word geometry, same locality profile; different licensed meaning. The case doesn't threaten dependency notation (Gibson pairs rules with a lexicon), but it shows where the explanatory burden has moved: from dependency geometry to the conventional inventory.

Selective satiation shows another dissociation. Lu, Frank, and Degen (2024) present a meta-analysis of 25 satiation experiments across seven English island types: adjunct, complex-NP, subject, *that*-trace, *want-for*, *whether*-island, and the Left Branch Condition. All involve extraction configurations standardly treated as island phenomena. Six of the seven satiate reliably, with acceptability rising between 0.005 and 0.030 units per repetition on a 0–1 scale, and subject-island ratings rising roughly one point on a seven-point scale across ten repetitions (Lu, Frank, & Degen, 2024, p. 14). The Left Branch Condition is the exception; its estimate of −0.0021 (95% CI [−0.0046, 0.0004]) is consistent with either no satiation or a negligible effect. If dependency locality were sufficient to explain the relevant island-judgment patterns, exposure should not split these configurations so sharply. That differential pattern suggests that locality is not the whole explanation.

Agreement attraction shows a further dissociation. In self-paced reading, a plural attractor (*cabinets* in *The key to the cabinets are on the table*) reduces the disruption that readers normally show at a mismatched verb; matched grammatical sentences with the same attractor show no comparable effect (Wagers, Lau, & Phillips, 2009). Phillips, Wagers, and Lau (2011) describe this as a grammatical asymmetry: attraction effects concentrate in ungrammatical strings and are largely absent from grammatical ones. The attractor rescues processing without rescuing grammar. A locality-only account of difficulty would predict disruption to track dependency length or retrieval burden more directly; the grammatical asymmetry points instead to licensing and retrieval interacting in construction-specific ways.

Taken together, these cases suggest that dependency locality is powerful, but not sovereign. Locality helps explain why some dependencies are costly. It doesn't explain why the same dependency skeleton can license one form–meaning pairing and block another, why repetition improves some island violations but not others, or why attraction effects concentrate in ungrammatical strings. The evidence supports the narrower claim Gibson himself reaches in his conclusion: dependency grammar as "the simplest formalism for how words are connected to one another within a sentence." Grammaticality asks more than whether the arrows can be drawn.
