## Abstract

Gibson (2026) argues that dependency grammar is the simplest theory of grammar, with dependency locality as the unifying principle behind processing effects and crosslinguistic word-order regularities. Locality has robust corpus support as a processing primitive, and the representational case for dependency formalisms over phrase structure plus transformations has real force. But the broader title claim reaches beyond what the locality literature itself supports. Three dissociations press against a single-factor story. English *I have 57 years* has the same dependency skeleton on a duration reading (licensed) and an age reading (not licensed in contemporary Standard English); the difference is conventional, not geometric. Six of seven English islands satiate reliably under repeated exposure in Lu, Frank and Degen's (2024) meta-analysis, but the Left Branch Condition does not — a contrast hard to accommodate on a single-factor account. Agreement-attraction effects concentrate in ungrammatical strings, a pattern memory-load alone does not predict. Gibson's own concluding remarks describe dependency grammar more narrowly, as the simplest formalism for word-to-word connection within a sentence; that narrower claim is the one the evidence supports, and grammaticality asks more than word-to-word connection.
